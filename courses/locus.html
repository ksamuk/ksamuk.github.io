Selection

Two ways to add selection: the s / h shortcut below, or set the three genotype fitnesses directly.
s = strength of selection against aa; h = dominance of a (0 recessive · 0.5 additive · 1 dominant). Sets w(AA)=1, w(Aa)=1−hs, w(aa)=1−s.
Editing these overrides s and h. Equal fitnesses = no selection; heterozygote highest = overdominance.

The model — scientific details

Dr. Kieran Samuk (Assistant Professor, UC Riverside). Version 1.0.0 (July 08 2026)

Locus is a forward-time Wright–Fisher simulation of one diallelic locus (alleles A and a, frequencies p and q = 1−p) in one or more finite diploid populations connected by migration.[2,3] Each generation applies, in a fixed order, the four classic evolutionary forces — selection → mutation → migration → genetic drift — and then re-samples the next generation of diploids. Every run is an independent, seeded pseudo-random realisation, so replicate runs show the distribution of outcomes, not a single deterministic trajectory.

The life cycle (one generation)

Genotypes are formed from the allele frequency p at an effective inbreeding level Feff (see below), giving a Hardy–Weinberg distribution deformed toward homozygotes:[1,5]

gAA = p² + Feffpq    gAa = 2pq(1−Feff)    gaa = q² + Feffpq

(Feff = 0 recovers exact Hardy–Weinberg proportions.)

  1. Selection. Genotype fitnesses reweight the zygotes; the mean fitness is w̄ = gAAwAA + gAawAa + gaawaa and the post-selection allele frequency is p′ = (gAAwAA + ½·gAawAa) / w̄.[3,11] Because inbreeding exposes recessive homozygotes, larger F speeds selection against a recessive allele.
  2. Mutation. Symmetric, recurrent mutation each generation: p′ = p(1−u) + (1−p)v, with u = v = μ. Applied every generation, so a fixed locus is pulled back off the boundary whenever μ > 0.[3,4]
  3. Migration. m is the fraction exchanged per connection. Stepping-stone (a line of neighbours): p′ = p + m(pd−1−p) + m(pd+1−p). Island (all-to-all): p′ = p + m·Σj≠d(pj−p). The result is clamped to [0, 1].[6,7]
  4. Genetic drift. With finite N, the next generation is drawn as N diploids from the (inbred) genotype distribution: nAA ~ Binomial(N, gAA), then nAa ~ Binomial(N−nAA, gAa/(gAa+gaa)), giving p′ = (2nAA+nAa)/2N and observed heterozygosity Hobs = nAa/N. With N = ∞ the step is deterministic (no drift) and Hobs = 2pq(1−Feff).[2,3] Sampling inbred diploids inflates the drift variance by (1+Feff) — i.e. inbreeding also shrinks the effective population size.

Non-random mating

Two controls feed the single effective inbreeding coefficient used above. A fixed inbreeding coefficient F is an instantaneous whole-population deviation from Hardy–Weinberg toward homozygotes.[5] Positive assortative mating α is the fraction of individuals mating with their own genotype; its homozygote excess builds up over generations through Fα ← α(1+Fα)/2, approaching the equilibrium α/(2−α).[4] The two combine as independent sources of homozygosity, Feff = 1 − (1−Fα)(1−F). Neither changes allele frequency on its own.

Diversity & differentiation statistics

  • He — expected heterozygosity 2pq, the Hardy–Weinberg value.[1]
  • Hobs — observed heterozygosity, the realised frequency of heterozygotes (≈ 2pq(1−Feff)).
  • F (FIS) — inbreeding coefficient within populations, 1 − Hobs/He (near 0 under random mating).[5,8]
  • FST — differentiation among populations. Within a replicate, HS = meand 2pdqd, HT = 2p̄(1−p̄), and FST = (HT−HS)/HT; the pairwise matrix uses the same formula for each pair.[8,9]

Assumptions & simplifications

  • Non-random mating is a whole-population deviation from Hardy–Weinberg, identical in every deme — not an individual mating mechanism or spatial structure.
  • Mutation is symmetric (one rate μ drives both directions); fitnesses are capped at 1, so heterozygote advantage is set by lowering the homozygotes.
  • Island migration is the pairwise generalisation p + m·Σj≠d(pj−p), not the classic migrant-pool form (1−m)p + mp̄; the two agree for small m.[6]
  • Drift for large N uses a normal approximation to the binomial; small-N draws are exact.
